What Joe Biden’s election victory means for environmentally-conscious consumers

With states across the United States certifying their votes, scientists across the country (and world) can breathe a collective sigh of relief as the inauguration of former Vice President Joe Biden and Senator Kamala Harris appears all but certain. The upcoming administration promises to take climate change seriously and has already outlined several actions to put the United States back into the climate conversation. The current administration has taken a very different, almost dismissive, approach to environmental policy. Come January, climate-conscious citizens will finally have a government that will work with them to solve these issues. But much has to be done to regain lost ground. What specifically has the Trump administration done, and what how does the Biden administration plan to minimize the environmental effects Trump has caused?

Trump administration’s environmental policies

President Donald Trump has always viewed environmental regulations as bad for the bottom-line, and thus his administration has handled environmental issues vastly differently than how the Biden administration will handle these same issues.

President Trump’s four years of environmental policy boil down to a few key concepts. First, his administration has repeatedly valued fossil fuel companies very highly, to the point where they often take precedence over environmental considerations like preservation of biodiversity ( keeping our air and waters clean, and protecting public lands). Trump has also repeatedly decried science as a whole, removing climate change from environmental agency websites, hiring climate-deniers to chief scientific positions, and cutting science research funding (and here). Trump also withdrew the United States from the Paris Climate Accord. Finally, he made it a mission to roll back environmental policies created by the Obama administration. These include (but are not limited to) completely rewriting the Clean Power Plan to be more corporate-friendly; cutting the EPA budget by 30%; and reducing regulations on pollution (DailyDot). In total, President Trump rolled back over one hundred environmental policies, with the goal of freeing corporations from governmental restrictions.

Biden’s Climate Action Plan

According to the Biden-Harris transition website BuildBackBetter, the President-Elect will make large investments in eight major sectors of the climate economy: Infrastructure, Auto Industry, Transit, Power Sector, Buildings, Housing, Agriculture and Conservation, and Environmental Justice. In summary, his plan involves aggressive federal spending on development of new (or improvement of existing) houses, buildings, and infrastructure, and on clean energy research and development. Biden-Harris also commit to creating jobs in sustainable agriculture and conservation and tackling environmental racism. He even appointed the first-ever “climate czar” in John Kerry, reaffirming his commitment to addressing climate change as a national security threat. Their strongest differentiator from President Trump’s environmental policies is focus on humanity / unity. The Biden administration wants every voice included when addressing climate change. This includes implementing policies put forth by progressive Democratic politicians like Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez. While Joe Biden has not fully endorsed the Green New Deal, he promises to implement parts of it, including increasing generation of renewable energy, increase fuel efficiency of vehicles, and invest in more sustainable buildings and homes (NYTimes).

Most scientists will agree that international cooperation is crucial for climate change mitigation, and this starts with a President that is committed to unity across -- and within -- party lines.

What does this mean for consumers?

For consumers that wish to enact a more sustainable lifestyle, President-elect Joe Biden’s pro-environmental policies will naturally encourage more businesses to run more sustainably, such as using only sustainably-harvested products. With small businesses feeling additional pressure from consumers to place more value on people and planet, and with President-elect Joe Biden’s intent to create a Small Business Fund, consumers can expect to see more small, environmentally-conscious businesses in their communities. This, compounded with his plan to invest in sustainable development and clean energy initiatives, will make finding local small businesses demonstrating some degree of sustainability much easier.





Plastic-free alternatives for your bathroom

It is no secret that plastics have simultaneously become civilization’s greatest solution and its greatest curse. They’re lightweight, versatile, and inexpensive, but because of this, plastics of many forms are found in products that you wouldn’t expect. Of course, the current pandemic does little to change this; N95, surgical, and even disposable paper masks are littered with plastic components. If every citizen in Britain wore one disposable mask every day for a year, approximately 73,000 tons of plastic waste would be produced. Consumers can make significant changes in their environmental impact by knowing what common household items contain plastics. Not only will replacing these items benefit the environment, doing so can also benefit your physical health.

In general, when thinking about reducing plastic waste, doing research on a product’s packaging is often a good step to finding brands that make plastic-free, all-natural products. Nevertheless, making an educated decision before adding a product to your cart is vital to taking control of your environmental impact. Websites like Life Without Plastic, EcoRoots, and Habitat are great sites to browse if you are curious about the zero-waste lifestyle.

However, the most sustainable product is the one you currently own. Use the products you already have until they run out or are no longer functional; only then does it make sense to switch to these plastic-free products.





Meat: Less is more to help the environment

A majority of humans are concerned about climate change but are unsure how to make an individual difference. Veganism has grown globally and has led to more awareness of the meat production process. Also, environmental concerns have more people buying plant-based meat alternatives. But what specifically about meat is so bad for the environment? Is it enough to simply buy local or “grass-fed” meat, or is the meat industry more nuanced?

Does Local Matter?

Food emits greenhouse gases (GHG) at every life stage, but the impact of each stage varies with the type of food. Meats by far have the highest environmental impact, while plants have the lowest. What makes meat more carbon-intensive than plants? The difference comes in land-use change and farming. As an example, let’s analyze the land use and farming required to produce beef, the largest GHG emitter. Cows need vast areas of grassland, which often need to be converted from forests and other natural ecosystems; cows grazing on deforested land contribute twelve times more GHG and use fifty times more land than those grazing on natural pastures. Thus, unsurprisingly, livestock is grown on more than 80% of the world’s farmland but produces less than 20% of the world’s calories. Cows also require a lot of fresh water and produce methane, a potent GHG. While beef is the most damaging, other meats are bad for the environment for similar reasons.

There are many people who would rather buy meat locally than remove meat from their diets. After all, doing this reduces unnecessary emissions by planes, ships, and trucks. However, transportation from producer to consumer explains less than 5% of food emissions, while land-use and farmland emissions make up more than 80% for most foods. Even grass-fed beef is much more carbon-intensive than plant-based foods. So it’s safe to say that consuming less meat, rather than local meat, will reduce your carbon footprint.

Squashing the Beef

While cutting out meat completely is too daunting of a lifestyle change for many, eating chicken instead of beef cuts one's food emissions by 25%. For meat lovers who cannot live without the taste of beef, there are other ways to reduce food emissions. Plant-based meats such as Beyond and Impossible taste like regular beef while requiring significantly less land and water to produce. Another option is eating less meat. Eating 25% less meat can save enough high-quality land to cover all of Indiana. Even easier, if every American went vegetarian for one day a week, we would save 100 billion gallons of water and reduce our carbon footprint by 1.2 million tons of CO2 annually.
